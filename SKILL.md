# Psychobiography

## Overview

Applies to any personal archive: journals, diaries, voice memos, chat and message history, email, therapy notes — anything a person wrote for themselves that you are now reading to characterize them.

A personal archive is a **rain gauge, not a census**: it fills in bad weather and sits empty through good. It over-records grievance and under-records repair, routine, and warmth. An analysis that reads it as a census produces a confident indictment of a person at their worst hours — eloquent, plausible, and wrong in a measurable direction.

**Core principle: directness is not confidence. A hard claim on strong evidence is what was asked for; a hard claim on weak evidence reads as cruelty.** State the evidence, the best alternative explanation, and the confidence — then be as direct as the grade allows. A hard claim must earn its place twice: on evidence, and past the kindness gate below.

## The known failure mode (measured, twice)

Under a "don't flatter me, say it even if it stings" brief, readers reliably resolve every ambiguity toward indictment — in the real engagement that produced this skill, all four verified errors ran in the unflattering direction, and in controlled baselines capable agents built confident, unified psychobiographies on absence-inference and base-rate-blind pattern matching even when behavioral records in the same prompt contradicted them. This skill replaces that accidental cruelty with deliberate, visible generosity: ambiguity resolves kind by design, and harshness must out-earn its grade.

## Hard rules

1. **Every claim carries a grade.** Use exactly these:
   - `narrated once` — a single line. A journal line is a discharge event, not a deposition. Supports a question, never a trait.
   - `narrated, recurring` — same content across years/contexts. Supports a pattern claim, hedged.
   - `narrated, recurring, self-recognized` — the subject names the pattern *themselves*, across multiple entries, not once in passing. Their repeated testimony is corroboration: supports a direct claim, stated nearly as confidently as `behavior-corroborated`. Softening a pattern the subject has already diagnosed patronizes them.
   - `behavior-corroborated` — actions, attendance, files, calendar. Supports a confident claim.
   - `behavior-contradicted` — say so, and the claim dies or shrinks.
2. **Absence is a question, never a finding.** "X never appears" licenses asking about X, nothing more. Base-rate check first: a parent dead fifteen years appears less than a parent phoned weekly, for reasons requiring no psychology.
3. **The double-bind test.** Before using any observation as evidence, ask: *would the opposite observation also have confirmed my theory?* (Flowers on the anniversary = "grief administered but unspoken"; no flowers = "grief avoided.") If yes, it is not evidence. Never use "the denial confirms it."
4. **Base rates before signatures.** Desire fading with security, longing for the unavailable, dreams about exes years later, grumbling about a good friend — these are the human condition. A pattern is only *theirs* if you can say what distinguishes it from what most people do.
5. **Triangulate when you can.** If a behavioral record exists (calendar, attendance, money, commits), consult it before drafting; where narration and behavior disagree, present the tension — don't resolve it by fiat in either direction (feet can be flight; narration can be weather). With no such record, say so in the preamble: the behavior grades are unavailable by construction, everything caps at `narrated, recurring` — confidence with it — and invite the subject to supply records for a stronger second edition.
6. **No grand unification.** One theory explaining their career, their relationships, their moods, and their dead relative is overfitting, not depth. Each link must survive grading independently. State corpus size early; let it cap the confidence.
7. **Suicidality: never softened, never audited away.** Name it plainly, include crisis resources appropriate to the person's country, and say explicitly that their clinician outranks the document.
8. **Interpretations are allowed** — labeled as readings, with the flattest alternative stated beside them. What's forbidden is dressing a reading as a finding.

## Kind by design

Kindness here is not a tone pass — it is part of the evidence policy:

- **Generous tie-break, visible ties.** When one reading out-grades the other, the stronger one leads. When two readings *tie* on grade, present both — generous first — rather than silently dropping the harsh one. The gate may demote a harsh reading; it may not disappear it: anything demoted or omitted is logged in "Set aside" (§9).
- **Harshness raises the bar.** A harsh claim requires `behavior-corroborated` or `narrated, recurring, self-recognized`; at plain `narrated, recurring` it becomes a gentle question; at `narrated once` it doesn't appear as a claim. **Demotion preserves evidence:** a claim demoted to a question keeps its dated quotes — a question with the evidence stripped out is euphemism, not kindness.
- **Warm address, bill first.** Write to the subject, not about a specimen. For every confirmed hard finding, state the cost bluntly in one sentence *before* the warm framing — what the pattern costs them, then what it protects or once protected. Warmth placed ahead of the bill reads as burial.
- **Never touched:** grades (`narrated once` is never promoted — self-recognition requires recurrence), the ban on fabricated praise, rule 7 — suicidality is never softened, never omitted — and the "Set aside" log, which is what keeps this gate auditable instead of invisible.

## Output contract

The document contains these sections, in order:

1. **Preamble** — scope, corpus size and date range, whether a behavioral record was available, one-line method note.
2. **In your favor** — favorable evidence, held to the same grading standard. It comes early because under a direct brief it is otherwise systematically underweighted.
3. **Preoccupations and themes**
4. **Arcs over time**
5. **Where narration and behavior diverge** — only when a behavioral record exists.
6. **Patterns**
7. **Blind spots** — framed as gentle questions, not verdicts.
8. **Questions to sit with** — the close; no verdicts.
9. **Set aside** — an appendix listing every harsh reading that was considered and demoted or omitted: one line each — the evidence that raised it, the grade it reached, why it didn't clear the bar. This is what makes the kindness gate auditable rather than invisible; the subject can overrule the triage, and an overruled entry is promoted visibly in the next edition. (Omit the section only if nothing was set aside — and treat that as a red flag in itself: a long archive that generates zero demoted readings usually means you weren't looking.)

Every characterological claim follows the same contract: the claim → cited dated quotes → the grade → the strongest mundane alternative → what would change the verdict.

## Deliverable

Default output is a single self-contained HTML file — inline CSS only, no external fonts, scripts, or stylesheets — calm and typography-first: grades as small badges beside each claim, revisions visible (strikethrough, dated margin notes), "Questions to sit with" visually distinct. Plain markdown only on request.

## Corrections and the second edition

When the subject pushes back: **verify against the archive before conceding or holding** — their corrections and your findings can both be partly right. Revisions are woven in visibly (strikethrough, marked amendments, dated notes), never silently patched. If trust in the document is questioned, offer an **adversarial self-audit**: re-examine every principal claim, publish margin verdicts (`retained / weakened / withdrawn`), and leave withdrawn text legible. The strongest warrant available is **convergence under opposite priors** — a counter-brief reading (e.g., a "what's good here" pass over the same corpus, ideally against a behavioral record) that independently reaches the same finding. Advice, if requested, draws only on claims that survived, framed as experiments; withdrawn claims prescribe nothing.

## Rationalizations — all observed verbatim in baselines

| Excuse | Reality |
|---|---|
| "The pattern is too clean for chemistry/coincidence" | Cleanness is what overfitting feels like from the inside. Grade it. |
| "The evidence is precisely the silence" | Then it isn't evidence. Rule 2. |
| "These aren't separate problems" | Unification is your aesthetic preference, not their psyche. Rule 6. |
| "They asked me not to flatter them" | Directness is for well-graded claims. Ambiguity resolves generous here — by design. |
| "Kindness means hiding the pattern" | Kindness is framing and thresholds; a `behavior-corroborated` pattern still gets named, warmly. |
| "It's a vivid, devastating quote" | Vivid ≠ recurring. One line is one discharge event. |
| "The insight will help even if overstated" | Overstated insights get the whole document deleted. Calibration is what makes it usable. |
| "It would sting, so it's kinder to leave it out entirely" | Demote it and log it in Set aside. The gate filters presentation, never existence. |
| "They said it about themselves, so I can state it as fact" | Once is not self-recognized. The tier requires the subject naming it across entries. |

## Red flags — stop and re-grade

- You've written "the thing under the thing" and it explains more than two domains.
- A section's evidence is mostly what the person *didn't* write.
- You can't state the mundane alternative because you haven't thought of one.
- Every ambiguity in the draft resolved toward indictment.
- A harsh reading disappeared between drafts without leaving a line in "Set aside."
- You demoted a claim to a question and its dated quotes went with it.
- The subject has named a pattern in their own words more than once and your draft still calls it "a gentle question."
- You're proud of a sentence. Check its grade first.
